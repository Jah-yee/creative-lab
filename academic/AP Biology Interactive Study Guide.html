Question 1: E. coli Gene Regulation

Scroll to animate

Question Components

a

Regulation Mechanism: Describe how the inducible promoter system controls gene expression and explain the roles of inducer sugar and repressor.

b

Data Analysis: Compare pigment production in normal versus promoter mutant bacteria with sugar present and explain what it indicates.

c

Experimental Design: Propose an experiment to determine minimum concentration of inducer sugar required for visible pigment production.

d

Gene Regulation Diagram: Draw a diagram showing the regulatory system in presence and absence of inducer sugar.

Sample Answers

In the inducible promoter system, gene expression is regulated through a repressor protein that controls transcription of the pigment genes. In the absence of inducer sugar, the repressor protein binds to the operator region, physically blocking RNA polymerase from transcribing the pigment genes. This creates a default "off" state where minimal pigment is produced (as shown by the 0.1 AU/mL baseline production).

When inducer sugar is added to the environment, it binds to the repressor protein, causing a conformational change that prevents the repressor from binding to the operator. This conformational change effectively removes the blockage, allowing RNA polymerase to bind to the promoter and initiate transcription of the pigment genes. Translation of the resulting mRNA leads to the production of enzymes that synthesize the purple pigment.

The inducer sugar thus acts as a molecular switch that turns gene expression "on" by inactivating the repressor, while the repressor serves as a negative control element that keeps the system turned "off" in the absence of inducer. This mechanism allows the bacterium to produce pigment only when the specific sugar is present in the environment, conserving energy and resources when the pigment is not needed.

The data shows a dramatic difference in pigment production between normal bacteria with inducer sugar (4.5 AU/mL) and promoter mutant bacteria with inducer sugar (0.2 AU/mL). This represents a 22.5-fold reduction in pigment production when the promoter is mutated.

This significant difference indicates that an intact, functional promoter is absolutely essential for effective gene expression, even when the repressor is inactivated by the inducer sugar. The promoter sequence contains critical binding sites for RNA polymerase and associated transcription factors. When mutated, these binding sites are likely altered or destroyed, preventing RNA polymerase from properly recognizing and binding to the DNA.

The small amount of pigment production in the promoter mutant (0.2 AU/mL) suggests that either: (1) the mutation did not completely abolish promoter function, allowing minimal transcription; or (2) there may be some basal transcription occurring from alternative, weaker promoters elsewhere in the DNA sequence. Regardless, the data clearly demonstrates that the intact promoter structure is necessary for the inducible system to function properly and produce significant levels of pigment.

Experimental Design:

  1. Prepare 10 test tubes containing equal volumes of the engineered E. coli culture.
  2. Create a concentration gradient of inducer sugar: 0, 0.001, 0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 5.0, and 10.0 mM.
  3. Add the appropriate volume of inducer stock solution to each tube to achieve these concentrations.
  4. Incubate all cultures under identical conditions (37Â°C with shaking) for 24 hours.
  5. After incubation, observe each tube visually to determine the lowest concentration at which purple pigment is visible to the naked eye.
  6. Measure pigment production quantitatively using a spectrophotometer at the appropriate wavelength for the purple pigment (likely 500-600 nm).
